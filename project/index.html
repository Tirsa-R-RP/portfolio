DE/REcontruct an App

Deconstruction

I created wireframes that are similar to the actual look of the app but only highlighted the text relevant to achieving the goal of applying for föräldrapenning (parental leave payments).

Two key ui patterns that users found very helpful for navigating through the site and achieving their goal, were Breadcrumbs and Catergorization. No matter where the user was in the process, they could always find their way around the site with these ui patterns.

Two ui patterns that some found frustrating in the application were the continuous scrolling and not being able to skip or come back to steps. There was so much information to be read and inputted, that it felt like it went on forever when scrolling through the app. They tried to address this with accordian menus, but there was still a lot of information overwhelming the user. Then while actively in the steps left wizard, the user did not have the option to stop and save their work to come back at a later time. They can’t even skip ahead to see what kind of information they will need for the next step. It would be helpful to be able to hop around the questions and get back to it once you have all the information at hand and time to finish all the steps.

Reconstruction

The app I deconstructed and reconstructed was the Fösäkringskassan app. Försäkringskassan is Sweden's social welfare agency that pays people when they are unable to work.

The goal I focused on was applying for parental leave compensation. A parent will need to complete this workflow in order to get paid while on maternity/paternity leave.

When selecting an app, I posted in a few Facebook forums for mothers in Sweden as well as contacted a few of my friends who are mothers. Most of them said they had issues with the Försäkringskassan app. Not only with applying for maternity leave, but also for VAB and other services. I explored the app and eventually decided on the Försäkringskassan app. With the specific goal of applying for maternity leave payments as my focus. It is a service that I had to use when I was on maternity leave, so I was familiar with the process already. I took some of their feedback into consideration and made a reconstruction.

The app doesn't make a good first impression, the first thing the user encounters when they open the app on their phone is a prompt that they will be redirected to the website.

Step 1: Start page

In the current app the user is notified that they will be redirected to the website outside of the app. In this reconstruction, the user can access the app without being prompted to go into their browser.

The user is already confronted with a mass of information that can be found after continuously scrolling. I would reconstruct it by creating an accordion menu that highlights the different topics all on one screen. If the user wants to learn more about that topic, they will have access to the vast amount of information provided.

Step 2: Förälder

Once the user selects the förälder (parent) link they are presented with the three most used services at the top. This allows the user to see what they most likely came to the app for, without having to search around the site.

But as the user currently scrolls down there are topic links with short descriptions of what can be found once they click on that link. But those links just lead to more links and descriptions. In my reconstruction, the user can see their options on one screen and pick which topic they really want to proceed to. That way most of the work is still done on this page, instead of jumping from link to link.

Step 3: Log in with BankID

In the pages leading up to this page, there are cards, then a list of links, then cards again. It is inconsistent in its display and layout. In this reconstruction the set up is more in line with the previous pages to keep things consistent.

I also added two more banking options in the app that are found on the full website version but are not currently offered in the app. That way if users need to log in using these methods, they can access them immediately rather than having to wait until they can sit in front of a computer.

Logging in with BankID is something that most people are familiar with in Sweden. It is used for most government services as well as with making payments, such as Swish. Therefore, the help and related links sections can be put into accordion menus. Users will most likely not need them, but if they do, they are easily accessible without adding to the cognitive load of most users.

Step 4: Log in part 2

In my reconstruction I would move the BankID process into the Försäkringskassan app. It would be much more straightforward and take a lot less effort if the BankID process could be embedded into the actual app.

Step 5: Log in part 3

As of now, the users need to close the application, find their BankID app on their phone, open it, input their personal numbers and pin codes, then navigate back to the original application. A lot of people purposely do not put their BankID app on the first page of their phone for safety reasons. This navigating through apps on a phone becomes more of an inconvenience and breaks the flow of the user experience.

One positive aspect of the BankID experience is the input feedback (or feedforward) in the input fields. This allows the user to know exactly how to input their codes and avoid mistakes.

Step 6: Ansök

This is where the parent starts the application process. They first must select which child they would like to apply for and fill in their income information.
There are currently small information buttons found by each header. However, they don’t provide any new information other than what is already stated on the page. In my reconstruction, I would remove some of the cognitive load by moving the explanation text into the information pop up. If a user needs more information, they can find it, but usually this is a process they will complete every month. After that initial onboarding period, the parent will not need to have the whole process explained every time.

I have also dimmed the information of the child that has reached their limit of days being taken. That will provide the user with some feedforward, that it is most likely the other child they are looking into. The other parent might still have days available to them, so removing the child from the list is not ideal. But this does let the user know that all their days were used up and allows them to focus on the child with days left.

Another change in this step left process, is that the continue button at the bottom lets the user continue onto the next page, even if they have not filled in all the information. By being able to skip to the next page, the user can see what information they will need to fill out the application, or just browse around for more information regarding planning. They might want to play around with the dates and hours to see what works best for them, without actually submitting the application.

Step 7: Ansök part 2

This page was very similar to the others with the cognitive load of information overwhelming the user. There are two areas where they restate which child you are applying for, even though you just selected which child on the previous page.
There is another information button that doesn’t provide any additional information than that which is already displayed.
Then there is another section that takes up a lot of space that tells the user that if they want their application to be processed, they must complete the later steps.

I removed the first section which restates which child you are picking days for, since it is found further down with that child’s personal number and a chart of days. I moved the information and “change an earlier application” sections into accordion menus, since they do not always need to be displayed. I then removed that unnecessary continue to finish section and moved the “estimated compensation” disclaimer information into the information button near the header. It is something that I assume needs to be posted, to let users know this is just a preliminary estimate, but it took up a lot of space and mental capacity to read the whole paragraph. When all the user really wants to do is pick days from a calendar picker and see how many days are left of each type of compensation. Instead of needing to scroll through a lot of text to get to the pertinent information.

Step 8: Ansök part 3

This page’s layout is very clear and concise, I would only change two aspects of the functionality.
I would add the ability for the user to skip ahead if they wanted to. As well as change the order of the “no” and “yes” on the last question. On the current app they change the order of the yes and no on the last question as opposed to the three previous questions.

I wonder what the reasoning was for chaning the order. It could be to help the users who might not be paying attention to click “yes” as the first option, because they do want to apply again after this application. But it could also be adverse to the user’s desires and lead them to click “no” by accident, preventing them from applying again. If they weren’t paying close attention, they might not notice that the order changed at the last moment.

Step 9: Ansök part 4

This is where the user can double check that all the information they entered, is correct. In this reconstruction, I would remove some of the excess and repetitive information. Such as where they restate the questions from the previous page, I left the topic and their answer.

There is a postal preference section that I removed. That seems like something that should be under “my pages” where the user sets up their account. That isn’t as relevant to their goal in this section of the site. But included an information button, so if the user wishes to change their postal preference, they know where to go in the set-up section of “my pages”.

I left the option to update a phone number and bank account, as those two things are important when applying for payment. But I moved the excess information into the information button near the header.

Analysis

Overall, I felt like Försäkringskassan has done a decent job with a difficult task. They are a government organization that deals with very important issues such as compensation when a person is unable to work. There can be a lot of emotions involved when a person is unable to work and needs to seek assistance from a government agency. Those emotions are with the user while they try to sift through all the information to reach their goal. Any frustrations or feelings of being overwhelmed by an application, can contribute to an already stressful personal situation.

There is a plethora of information that the user and Försäkringskassan are required to provide. Which results in a mass of information that the user needs to navigate through to get to what is relevant to them and their needs. They provide the user with many tips, information buttons, feedforward as well as input feedback. This makes inputting information easier, as well as decreases the chance of the user making a mistake. They also provide plenty of ways for the user to find out where they are at and find their way back to where they started using; breadcrumbs, navigation tabs, a home link, and search option.

What I tried to achieve in my reconstruction is to relieve some of the cognitive load by taking out redundant information. I tried giving the user options. The option to choose if they want to be exposed to certain information or not. To decide what is relevant to them, and if it isn’t, then they don’t need to click the accordion menu. The option to input or not input information, but still be allowed to move along the step left process to see the possibilities. By filtering out text and not overwhelming the user and giving them options. They will hopefully feel like they are more in control of the situation and not simply inputting information because they must. But feel like they are choosing to put in this information because it helps them reach their goal. Thus, resulting in a better experience and more online applications as opposed to paper or in-person applications submitted to Försäkringskassan.
